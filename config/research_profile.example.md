# Research Profile (template)

> This file defines what the relevance judge treats as signal. It is plain prose
> + concrete examples, reloaded every run, and sent to the scoring model as a
> cached system block. **The real `research_profile.md` is gitignored** (it is
> personal / grant-derived); copy this template to `config/research_profile.md`
> and fill it in with your own program. The judge reads the file verbatim, so
> write it the way you would brief a new lab member on "what I'd want to see."

## (a) What I work on

Describe your research program in prose: the systems, molecules, methods, and
biological contexts you care about. Be specific — gene/complex names, assays,
model systems, disease contexts. Example shape:

> I am a <field> researcher. My program centers on <topic/molecule/complex> and
> its <subunits/targets>. I study <mechanism/question> using <methods>. The
> biological contexts I care about most are <contexts>.

## (b) What I want to see in a digest

List the kinds of papers that are a clear "keep":
- New mechanistic findings about <your core topic>
- Method papers for <assays you use>
- <model system> studies of <process>
- ...

## (c) What I do NOT want (near-misses)

List adjacent-but-off-program work that should score low, so the judge learns the
boundary (this is what separates signal from firehose):
- Pure <adjacent subfield> with no connection to <your topic>
- Reviews / teaching / commentary (unless landmark)
- <technique> papers in unrelated systems
- ...

## (d) Concrete "would want" examples

A handful of real titles you would have wanted in a digest, ideally with a one-line
why. These anchor the score scale far better than prose alone:

- "<paper title>" — why it's squarely in-program.
- "<paper title>" — adjacent but relevant because <reason>.
- ...
